Important contradiction: assistant physician
This is the clearest discrepancy in the document.
For 4,968 insurance bill-level pairs where the assistant-physician and primary-physician lines could be compared on the same bill:

Non-GIPSA median: 50%
Only about 0.5% were near 10%.
The assistant-physician service DM163 is behaving like a tariffed visit charge—usually half the primary visit rate—not like 10% of the physician’s overall professional fee.
Cash is less consistent: only 18 comparable cases, with a median of 15% and doctor-specific variation.
Therefore:

Cash: use doctor-specific/fixed-charge configuration.
Do not implement a universal 10% rule.
DMO findings
Across more than 10,000 admissions containing DMO charges:
Only about 38–42% had exactly one DMO unit for every normalized stay day.
Approximately 70–76% were either exact or one unit short.
Approximately 86–92% were within two units of the normalized stay.
This pattern suggests “once per eligible occupied day,” often excluding an admission/discharge boundary or days in which the relevant service was not applicable.
The builder should calculate:
DMO units = eligible chargeable days
It should not simply use:
DMO units = date of discharge − date of admission

GIPSA insurance
Use the same configurable 100/50/25 framework as the starting policy, but keep the reduction schedule tariff/agreement-specific. The current historical matching is insufficient to certify that every TR290 route consistently uses 50% for the second package.
GIPSA exclusions remain separately billable only when allowed by the applicable package agreement.
Package plus open treatment
The document’s intended branching is sound:
If the package is the primary/higher treatment: retain the package and calculate the secondary open treatment using the applicable multiple-treatment reduction.
If the open treatment is primary/higher: treat the insurer submission as open billing unless the TPA requests package segregation.
However, this cannot be safely calculated from admission-level labels alone. We need a component ledger identifying:
Each treatment.
Package/open status.
Full eligible value.
Same/different sitting.
Related service and PF lines.
Inclusion/exclusion status.
Until that exists, package-plus-open cases should be routed correctly but the secondary amount should remain reviewable.
Medical plus surgery
Open surgery: assign medical-period and surgical-period charges to their respective treatment components.
Package surgery: include the surgical package, exclude package-covered items, and separately calculate medical-management-period items only where contractually allowed.
Do not classify every pre-operative medical line as separately billable medical management.
Day/charge bifurcation should be evidence-based, using service dates, OT dates, admission notes and package start/end.

Local anaesthesia
The rule should be:
Verified local anaesthesia: no anaesthetist PF.
Verified GA/regional/sedation: apply the corresponding rule.
Unknown anaesthesia type: unresolved—do not infer zero.
Fourteen cases had procedure wording explicitly suggesting local anaesthesia; eight still had anaesthetist charges. Review showed several clinically inconsistent combined procedure descriptions, proving that procedure names alone are not reliable enough to determine anaesthesia type.
Multiple surgeons
Statements such as “try to keep total surgeon PF within 35–40%” and “based on doctor discretion” are not deterministic billing rules.
For the builder:
Show the calculated individual PF components.
Apply a configurable soft warning at 35–40%.
Require a doctor-specific or approved override to enforce a cap.
Do not silently cap the amount.
Extended-stay logic
This is one of the strongest areas of historical support.
After package LOS:
Primary surgeon: one applicable visit per excess day.
Primary physician: one ward visit per day or two ICU visits per day.
Cross-consultants: applicable tariffed visits, normally one per consultant per day unless separately supported.
Use the actual excess ward/ICU day distribution.
The 97% historical presence rate strongly supports including this automatically in FC estimates. Quantity reconciliation is less exact because many historical professional lines contain bill_at but not the true service_at; the logic should therefore be based on the room-day ledger rather than bill-entry date.
